Lower Carbon Living

In the Air
For those who fly (frequently or otherwise) the big question to ask is “Do I need to fly?”, followed by “Is there another way I can get where I want to go?” Reducing the number of flights we take, or not flying at all, is the number one way of shrinking our carbon footprint. This applies equally to personal flying and business miles – although we may have less control over our business travel.

This is always going to be a hard area to deal with, particularly if we are travelling to see family and friends – what some people call “love miles”. Skype and other online video conferencing services are a poor substitute – but may be good alternatives in some situations.

On the Ground
The best way to get about is under our own steam – walking or cycling are great for local journeys, keeping you fit and healthy, connected with your neighbors, saving money, and often time too. For longer journeys, public transport is best (look for journey planning websites to find the best way of linking bus and train journeys).

A lifecycle “cradle to grave” analysis from the US suggests that when you factor in the emissions from manufacturing and disposing of a car, its emissions per mile are approximately double the fuel emissions alone.

Food

Our food accounts, on average, for between 20% and 30% of our annual carbon footprint. This is due to its manufacture and transport – from field to plate. Research from the University of Oxford estimates the footprints of different diets.

Diet Type Daily Meat Consumption
High meat-eaters (≥100 g/day) 2,624
Medium meat-eaters (50–99 g/day) 2,055
Low meat-eaters (<50 g/day) 1,705
Fish-eaters 1,427
Vegetarians 1,391
Vegans 1,055

Many of us already eat more than the “high meat-eaters”, so simply reducing the amount of meat (particularly red meat) that we eat will have a significant impact on our footprint (not to mention our pocket, and maybe our waistline…).
